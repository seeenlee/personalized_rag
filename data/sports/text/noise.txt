In Baseball, a grand slam occurs when a batter hits a home run while all three bases are occupied by teammates.

In Tennis, the term 'Love' is used to represent a score of zero, believed to originate from the French word 'l'oeuf' meaning egg.

In Cricket, a 'century' is scored when a batsman reaches 100 runs in a single innings without being dismissed.

In Rugby Union, a 'try' is worth five points and is scored by grounding the ball in the opponent's in-goal area.

In Golf, an 'eagle' refers to completing a hole in two strokes under par, while a 'birdie' is one stroke under par.

In Volleyball, a 'libero' is a defensive specialist who wears a different colored jersey and cannot serve or block.

In Swimming, the four competitive strokes are freestyle, backstroke, breaststroke, and butterfly.

In Formula 1 racing, the 'DRS' (Drag Reduction System) allows a trailing car to open a flap in the rear wing to increase top speed.

In Boxing, a 'knockout' (KO) is declared when an opponent is unable to rise from the canvas within a ten-second count.

In Gymnastics, the 'Vault' involves a sprint down a runway followed by a leap onto a springboard and a flight over the table.

In Badminton, the 'shuttlecock' or 'birdie' is a high-drag projectile that can reach speeds of over 300 km/h.

In Table Tennis, a 'let' occurs if the ball touches the net during a serve but otherwise lands legally in the opponent's court.

In Mixed Martial Arts (MMA), a 'submission' occurs when a fighter signals their defeat by tapping the floor or their opponent.

In Cycling, the 'Peloton' is the large main group of riders that stay close together to save energy by drafting.

In Archery, the center of the target is known as the 'X-ring' or 'bullseye,' and hitting it grants the maximum 10 points.

In Surfing, 'duck diving' is a technique used by surfers to push their board underwater and dive under oncoming waves.

In Water Polo, players are not allowed to touch the bottom of the pool and must 'eggbeater' kick to stay afloat.

In Fencing, the three weapons used are the Foil, the Epee, and the Sabre, each having its own specific target area.

In Bowling, a 'strike' is achieved when all ten pins are knocked down on the first delivery of a frame.

In Lacrosse, the 'crease' is the circular area around the goal that attacking players are generally not allowed to enter.

In Figure Skating, a 'Lutz' is an edge jump where the skater enters from a back outside edge and taps the ice with the other toe.

In Snowboarding, 'Goofy' stance refers to riding with the right foot forward, while 'Regular' is with the left foot forward.

In Wrestling, a 'pin' or 'fall' occurs when a wrestler holds both of their opponent's shoulders to the mat simultaneously.

In Athletics (Track and Field), a 'false start' occurs if an athlete moves from the starting blocks before the gun is fired.

In Polo, players use long-handled mallets to hit a ball while riding horses, with four players on each team.

In Squash, the ball is made of rubber and must be hit against the front wall; it can bounce only once on the floor.

In Rowing, 'sculling' involves using two oars per person, while 'sweeping' involves using only one oar per person.

In Equestrian Dressage, the horse and rider perform a series of predetermined movements to demonstrate balance and flexibility.

In Triathlon, athletes compete in three consecutive stages: swimming, cycling, and long-distance running.

In Ski Jumping, athletes are judged on their flight distance and their landing style, aiming for a 'Telemark' finish.

In Billiards (Pool), the '8-ball' must be the final ball potted after a player has cleared their designated set of stripes or solids.

In Darts, the highest scoring area is the 'triple 20,' which counts for 60 points, more than the bullseye's 50.

In Sumo Wrestling, a 'Rikishi' loses the match if any part of his body other than the soles of his feet touches the ground.

In Ultimate Frisbee, players score by catching a disc in the opposing team's end zone; running with the disc is not allowed.

In Handball (Olympic), the goalkeeper is the only player allowed inside the 6-meter goal area.

In Nascar, 'drafting' is the technique of following closely behind another car to reduce wind resistance and increase speed.

In Field Hockey, players use sticks with a rounded side and a flat side, and the ball can only be hit with the flat side.

In Karate, 'Kata' is a sequence of choreographed movements that simulate a fight against imaginary opponents.

In Judo, the 'Ippon' is the highest score a fighter can achieve, awarded for a throw with speed, force, and control.

In Sailing, 'tacking' is a maneuver where the bow of the boat is turned through the wind to change direction.

In Australian Rules Football, a 'mark' is awarded when a player cleanly catches a kicked ball that has traveled over 15 meters.

In Curling, 'sweepers' use brushes to melt the ice in front of the stone to influence its speed and trajectory.

In Skateboarding, an 'Ollie' is a fundamental trick where the rider and board leap into the air without using hands.

In Bobsleigh, the 'pilot' steers the sled while the 'brakeman' is responsible for stopping the sled after the finish line.

In Netball, players are restricted to specific areas of the court depending on their assigned position (e.g., Goal Shoot).

In Motocross, 'scrubbing' is a technique where riders stay low over jumps to maintain speed and minimize air time.

In Canoeing, a 'C-1' involves a single paddler kneeling in the boat and using a single-bladed oar to propel themselves.

In Taekwondo, points are awarded for kicks to the head and trunk, with spinning kicks receiving higher scores.

In Snooker, the highest possible break under standard conditions is 147, achieved by potting all reds with blacks and then all colors.

In Modern Pentathlon, athletes compete in five events: fencing, swimming, show jumping, pistol shooting, and running.
