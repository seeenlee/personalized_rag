A goal is awarded when the puck completely crosses the red goal line between the vertical posts and under the crossbar.

The goal line is a 2-inch wide red line painted across the ice, used to determine both goals and icing violations.

A hand ball violation occurs if a player closes their hand on the puck or bats it to a teammate in the offensive zone.

The entry draft allows teams to select amateur players; the order is determined by a weighted lottery for non-playoff teams.

Substitutions (line changes) occur on the fly or during stoppages, provided the exiting player is within 5 feet of the bench.

A block involves a player sliding or dropping to the ice to intercept a shot puck with their shin guards or body.

A personal foul, such as tripping or hooking, results in a minor penalty and a 2-minute stay in the penalty box.

A standard period of play lasts 20 minutes of stop-time; there are three periods in a regulation game.

The captain wears a 'C' and is the only player permitted to discuss rule interpretations with the officials.

Out of bounds occurs when the puck leaves the rink or hits the overhead netting above the glass.

A technical violation, like 'Too Many Men,' occurs if a sixth skater touches the puck during a transition.

A hat trick is recorded when a single player scores three goals in a game, often resulting in fans throwing headwear.

Playoff overtime is continuous 5-on-5 sudden death until a goal is scored; there are no shootouts in the postseason.

The rink dimensions are typically 200 feet by 85 feet with rounded corners to maintain puck momentum.

The defensive zone is the area between the blue line and the end boards where a team defends their own net.

Scoring from a distance is common via a 'slap shot' from the point, often through heavy traffic or screens.

A turnover is a loss of puck possession, frequently caused by a botched breakout or a successful poke check.

Video review is used to determine if a puck crossed the line before the whistle or if a play was offside.

Unsportsmanlike conduct results in a minor penalty for verbal abuse of officials or excessive 'diving.'

A fast break in hockey is known as an 'odd-man rush,' such as a 2-on-1, where attackers outnumber defenders.

Legal contact is defined as 'body checking,' which must be delivered with the trunk of the body against the shoulder.

A double-header is rare in modern schedules but historically refers to two games played back-to-back on one day.

A free shot, or 'penalty shot,' is awarded if a player is fouled from behind while on a breakaway.

A shutout (clean sheet) is recorded when a goaltender prevents the opposing team from scoring any goals.

A save is credited when a goalie prevents a puck that would have otherwise entered the net from crossing the line.

Icing is called when a player shoots the puck from behind the center red line across the opposing goal line.

Offside occurs when both skates of an attacking player completely cross the outer edge of the blue line before the puck.

Minor penalties last 2 minutes, Majors last 5, and Misconducts are 10 minutes without shorthanded play.

A power play ends immediately if the team with the man-advantage scores a goal during a minor penalty.

The crease is a blue semi-circle; goals are disallowed if an attacking player interferes with the goalie's ability to move.

Face-offs occur at nine designated dots on the ice, where two opposing centers battle for puck possession.

The trapezoid is the area behind the goal line where goalies are legally allowed to play the puck.

A slap shot uses a full backswing for maximum velocity, while a wrist shot prioritizes a quick, deceptive release.

A delayed penalty is signaled by the referee raising an arm; play continues until the offending team touches the puck.

Boarding is a penalty called when a player checks an opponent violently into the boards from behind or from a distance.

Enforcers are players tasked with physical play and protecting stars; 'grinders' focus on high-effort board battles.

The penalty kill 'box' or 'diamond' formation is a defensive strategy used to clog passing lanes while shorthanded.

A one-timer occurs when a player shoots a pass immediately without stopping the puck to settle it.

Standard minor penalties are 120 seconds in duration, but end early if a power play goal is conceded.

The 'Original Six' refers to the Bruins, Blackhawks, Red Wings, Canadiens, Rangers, and Maple Leafs.

Line changes 'on the fly' require the player entering the ice to be within 5 feet of the bench before the other exits.

The puck line is a point spread in hockey, usually set at 1.5 goals to create more betting parity.

High-sticking above the shoulder results in a penalty; if it causes blood, it is a 4-minute double-minor.

The Zamboni is a resurfacing machine that scrapes the top layer of ice and lays down a fresh coat of heated water.

In the regular season, a tie after overtime leads to a shootout involving three designated skaters per team.

Hockey equipment like shin guards and blockers are made of high-density foam and molded plastic for impact protection.

The neutral zone trap is a defensive scheme designed to clog the middle of the ice and force turnovers at the blue line.

Pinching occurs when a defenseman leaves their post at the blue line to challenge an opponent deep in the offensive zone.

The Hart Memorial Trophy is the annual award given to the player judged to be the most valuable to his team.

During 4-on-4 play, the ice is more open, allowing for more creative puck movement and higher-scoring chances.
